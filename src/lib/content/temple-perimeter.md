You skirt the outer wall of the complex, away from the gates and the monks and Tenzin's unblinking smile. The path narrows quickly—maintained stonework giving way to bare rock, then to a ledge barely wider than your shoulders. The mountain drops away to your left. Below, the clouds sit thick and white and absolute, like a floor that isn't.

You should turn back. Anyone sensible would turn back.

Instead, you press your spine against the wall and shuffle sideways, fingers searching for holds in the carved stone. The wind picks up. Prayer flags snap overhead—the last of them, this far out, sun-bleached and fraying. Beyond them, nothing. Just rock and sky and the faint smell of snow.

Then you see it.

A rope ladder, bolted to the cliff face with iron stakes driven deep into the rock. It drops straight down, past the ledge, past the last visible outcrop, and disappears into the cloud layer below. The ropes are thick and weather-darkened, but not frayed. Someone maintains this. The iron stakes are free of rust. The knots are tight and deliberate—the work of someone who knows what a failed knot means at this altitude.
